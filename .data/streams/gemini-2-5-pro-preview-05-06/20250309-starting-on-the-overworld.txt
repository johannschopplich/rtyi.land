## Game Development Process
- **Overall Progress:** The game "Return to Yoshi's Island" (RTYI) is in a late stage of development. All 15 main courses are complete.
- **Current Focus:** Development is currently centered on the overworld (which will have 14-15 distinct parts), remaining side levels, and incorporating reworks from collaborator Biobank.
- **Overworld Development Strategy:** Kaze plans to design all overworld parts simultaneously as a basic blockout, then adjust them. The process involves:
    1. Rough draft/blockout of all areas.
    2. Implementation of gameplay elements.
    3. Beta testing.
    4. Visual polishing and making them "look nice."
- **Beta Testing:**
    - Beta testing for RTYI is planned to start "pretty soon," possibly before the overworld is entirely finished.
    - The initial RTYI beta will involve sending demos with single levels to testers for iterative feedback and improvement.
    - Kaze is also starting beta testing for another, unrelated game called "Direct Mario."
- **Milestones:**
    - Completion of all 15 main courses is a major milestone.
    - The next significant milestone appears to be a functional, draft version of the entire overworld.

## Personal Insights

## Game Design Decisions/Challenges
- **Overworld Design & Scale:**
    - A recurring challenge is getting the scale and layout of overworld sections right so they are engaging and not "too fucking big" or "unfun to walk around."
    - The goal for the initial overworld area is "to build intrigue in the player" and "Ignite the desire... to want to explore the next part."
    - Iterative design is evident, with Kaze frequently adjusting layouts, sizes, and element placements based on "how it feels."
    - The overworld will feature 14-15 interconnected parts, compared in complexity more to Banjo-Kazooie than SM64.
- **Non-Deterministic RNG:**
    - Kaze created a new, faster random number generation function.
    - An unintended consequence is that it "kind of uses like true noise on accident so it may be impossible to test this game" (specifically for Tool-Assisted Speedruns due to lack of determinism, even across save states on emulators and real hardware).
- **Tutorialization and Player Guidance:**
    - Emphasis on subtle tutorialization: the first swimming experience is in shallow water; a Cheep Cheep holding a sign is planned to teach swimming controls.
    - Initial overworld design aims to make the player feel "stuck" briefly to encourage exploration before finding the first course (Bunny Brushwoods). Course 1's entrance (a barrel) might be blocked by a Pachyderm, requiring coins or knowledge of ground-pounding to pass.
- **Mario's Model & Animations:**
    - Mario's model is still being reworked. This is a reason for not immediately fixing certain visual bugs (like the hat texture when Mario takes it off), as the fix would need to be redone.
- **Progression and Unlocks:**
    - The overworld will change as the player progresses (e.g., a Bowser door requiring 4 stars, a Course 2 door requiring 1 star).
    - A "message in a bottle" event is planned after 2-3 stars, leading to a secret star.
- **Performance:** Kaze emphasizes caring about performance, aiming for the game to run well on N64 hardware, contrasting with some modern unoptimized games.

## Business Aspects
- **Funding Model:**
    - The game's development is costly, with Kaze stating, "The game as a whole is going to cost me in the tens of thousands."
    - Funding comes from his Patreon, YouTube channel (ad revenue, sponsorships), and donations.
    - He mentions the possibility of starting a "donation drive" to cover costs, particularly for paying collaborators like Biobank.
- **Monetization of Content:**
    - Kaze waits for sponsors to release some of his planned YouTube videos, as he needs to pay his video editor.
    - He humorously mentions product placement as potentially "the only way to monetize romhacking."
- **Release/Distribution:**
    - Primarily a ROM hack for N64 emulators/hardware.
    - There's a passing thought about a potential PC port: "There's a consideration to strip this off copyright stuff and, like, put it out as a PC game as well. So, like, everyone can just play it without needing an emulator."
    - Kaze mentions potentially making an OST cartridge if he buys music rights.
