## Game Development Process
- The game "Return to Yoshi's Island" has been in development for "almost three years."
- Current development focuses on refining existing areas (e.g., "Chris 1," the first overworld), implementing new features like tutorial systems, and ongoing engine upgrades.
- A significant portion of the stream was dedicated to designing and implementing "tutorial bunnies" to teach players new movement mechanics (dive jump, wall kick/slide, spin jump).
- Work includes visual updates (shading, textures for windmills, mushrooms), addressing bugs (softlocks, old "crusty" textures), and optimizing performance (sine/cosine functions, aiming for 60 FPS on N64).
- The engine upgrades are mostly complete, but the project is still considered a "journey" and not finished, estimated at "like 70 percent maybe."
- Kaze mentions progress on a separate Banjo-Kazooie ROM hack, specifically finishing a boss fight.

## Personal Insights
- Kaze expresses a pragmatic and somewhat cynical view of game development: "it always starts like really fun and eventually it turns into pure torture that's just the cycle of making games."
- He is acutely aware of the challenges of ROM hack development, particularly player patience and the lack of initial financial commitment from players: "But for a romhack, you can't really go over your patience budget... So there's no initial commitment from the player."
- Kaze is considering switching his streaming platform from Twitch to YouTube due to frustrations with Twitch's ad policies, stating, "Twitch ads are so terrible. I just quit using Twitch practically."
- He values clarity and accessibility in game design, especially for tutorials: "The tutorials need to be the most understandable way possible."
- Kaze reflects on the high failure rate of major ROM hack projects ("below four percent") and advises starting with smaller projects.

## Game Design Decisions/Challenges
- **Tutorial Design:** A major focus, with Kaze iterating on how to best teach new moves. This includes:
    - Deciding on "tutorial bunnies" as the delivery mechanism.
    - Debating the presentation: initially text, then signs due to N64 resolution limits ("The N64 does not have the resolution for pop-up text").
    - Designing visual cues (speech bubbles with button icons and arrows) and iterating on their clarity.
    - Implementing Biobak's suggestion of "rings for players to go through with the specified move to get the coin" to ensure players learn effectively.
    - Animating the bunnies to visually demonstrate the moves, followed by a particle ring objective.
- **N64 Technical Limitations:** Constantly influences design. Examples include texture resolution (64x64 for tutorial prompts, 16 colors for indexed textures), alpha channel limitations, and avoiding performance-heavy elements.
- **Camera Control:** Kaze emphasizes a player-centric camera philosophy: "if you make the camera rotate without the player being able to predict it, you're always gonna have to play a whole direction that they don't intend... That's why we never actually have the camera do anything without the player somehow forcing that." He struggles with forcing the camera perspective during tutorial cutscenes.
- **Movement System:** The game features an enhanced movement system, which Kaze claims is "even better than the one in Peach's Fury." Tutorials are crucial for teaching these advanced mechanics.
- **Level Design (Chris 1):** This area is being refined to be a "nice first course," with adjustments made to make jumps easier and to integrate the new tutorials.
- **Mario's Lighting:** Kaze acknowledges Mario's model appears "a bit too dark" and considers solutions like making the light brighter on him.
