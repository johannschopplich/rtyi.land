## Game Development Process
- **Project Status:** The game is described as being "pretty close to completion."
- **Current Focus:** The stream's work focuses on the overworld map, specifically modeling the terrain to connect different areas (forests, mountains, caves) and creating special visual effects like a "rainbow scroll effect" for a cave entrance.
- **Major Milestones:** Several key worlds are complete or nearly complete. Kaze mentions, "The craft world is basically done" and "They actually already finished the world that comes after that which is um space civilization world."
- **Technical Foundation:** The entire project is built upon the Mario 64 decompilation. Kaze uses Blender with custom Python scripts to model levels and export them directly into the game, a process he notes "enables all of this."

## Personal Insights
- **Design Philosophy:** Kaze believes in creating quality, intentional work. He feels it's "wrong to on purpose produce bugs" and dislikes the idea of knowingly leaving them in. He also feels that creators should earn the right to discuss their design process: "If you make a bad game and then talk about your game's design, that always feels super weird to me... But if you made the banger, and then you're like, yeah, this is how I made a banger, that feels good."
- **Self-Criticism & Iteration:** Kaze is highly critical of his own work in progress, stating, "I need to do something about this forest though. This forest is really, really bad. I don't like the forest at all." This shows a willingness to identify and rework elements until he is satisfied.
- **Personal Toll:** Kaze mentions the physical and mental strain of the project, stating, "I've been feeling super dizzy all day, I think something might be really wrong with me," and ends the stream early because he is "feeling pretty exhausted for today."

## Game Design Decisions/Challenges
- **Aesthetic Direction:** Design choices are heavily influenced by creating a specific "vibe." For a cave, Kaze wants "weird rainbow cave spirals" because it "fits the vibe of Dino Domain super well. Like it goes magical." For the world's color palette, he struggles to articulate the exact need, using evocative but non-technical terms: "it should be more like caramel chocolate pudding... it's like cotton candy it's like sweet."
- **Technical Constraints:** Development is a constant negotiation with the N64's hardware limitations. This is seen in discussions about texture memory ("we can do two textures at once but they have to be a lower resolution") and polygon counts for models ("that's 12 triangles," "try to stay below 18").
- **Problem Solving:** A significant portion of the stream is dedicated to solving a technical art problem: creating a seamless, tiling rainbow texture rotated at a 45-degree angle. This involves a process of intuition, mathematical calculation ("square root of 2 multiplied by 64"), and trial and error.
- **Level Flow & World Building:** Kaze works on logically connecting disparate geographic areas, figuring out how to transition from a forest area to mountains and how to visually block the player's view of unfinished sections using walls and trees.

## Business Aspects
- **Commercial Release Plans:** Kaze explicitly states his intention to potentially release the game commercially on Steam. "There's a chance I will turn this into a non-mario game like replace all the mario ip and put it on steam as well."
- **Legal Justification:** He believes this is possible specifically because of the decompilation project. "It should be legally possible because of the recompilation," which allows for the complete removal and replacement of all copyrighted Nintendo assets.
- **Marketing Strategy:** Kaze has already identified a key marketing angle for a potential commercial release: "Just imagine the promotional power of being like Yeah in 2025. No one optimizes their games anymore, but we actually optimized our game so much. It literally runs on a nintendo 64."
